**Where Should Customer Success Report, and Why Does It Matter?**

---

**After Reading This Chapter, You Will Be Able To:**

•	Evaluate the four primary CS reporting structures (CEO, COO, CRO, CCO) against the specific context of your organization

•	Calculate whether your expansion revenue percentage warrants CRO alignment

•	Identify the hidden costs of misaligned CS reporting, and quantify the enterprise value at stake

•	Build a business case for restructuring CS using data that executives and boards will find compelling

•	Design a 7-12 month implementation roadmap that avoids the pitfalls derailing 70% of organizational changes

---

**Opening Thesis**

**Where you place Customer Success in your organization determines whether the function becomes a strategic asset or a frustrated cost centre.** The reporting structure you choose shapes team mission, operational effectiveness, and sustainable revenue growth. There is no "best" model that works in all cases, but there is a best model *for your company* based on your stage, revenue composition, and strategic priorities. Get this decision wrong, and you will watch talented CSMs burn out while customers churn in silence.

---

**Context & Stakes: The $2 Billion Wake-Up Call**

Think about what happened at Salesforce. The leadership team realized their Head of Customer Success controlled $2 billion in revenue while their VP of Sales managed $1 billion. That was not a rounding error; it was a fundamental shift in how software businesses create value.

In subscription models, the executive responsible for *keeping* customers often owns more revenue than the one *acquiring* them. Yet many companies still structure CS as if it were a glorified support function.

![Salesforce Customer Success Case Study](images/Chapter%202%20Images/Chp%202%20Salesforce%20Case%20Study.png)

*Figure 2.4: Salesforce Customer Success transformation*


The stakes are substantial:

- Organizations with proper CS positioning achieve **10-20% higher retention rates**
- Expansion revenue differences of **20-40%** are observed between teams that are structured well and those that are not
- Enterprise value differentials of **$100M-$300M** have been documented for typical SaaS companies

These are not theoretical concerns. The gaps compound on an annual basis, creating widening performance differentials versus competitors whose structures have been optimized for retention and expansion.

**The industry is in a period of active experimentation.** Research from 2025 shows:

- **43%** of CS teams report to the CEO (down from 50%)
- **33%** report to the CRO (up 38% year-over-year)
- **15%** report to the COO
- **9%** report to other executives

![CSM Team Reporting Structure](images/Chapter%202%20Images/CSM%20Team%20Reporting%20Structure.png)

*Figure 2.1: Current CSM team reporting structures across the industry*


This migration toward revenue-aligned structures reflects growing recognition that CS is a strategic revenue driver. Context matters a great deal, however; CRO reporting is not "better" than CEO reporting in every case. The right choice depends on factors that are unique to each organization, factors like growth stage, the proportion of revenue generated through expansion, and the cross-functional influence the CS team requires to be effective.

**The Core Framework: Four Reporting Models**

Each model optimizes for different organizational priorities. Understanding the trade-offs enables informed decisions.

![CSM Reporting Models Framework](images/Chapter%202%20Images/Chp%202%20CSM%20Reporting%20Models%20Framework.png)

*Figure 2.2: The four CSM reporting models framework*


**Model 1: CEO Reporting, Strategic Elevation**

**Best for:** Early-stage companies (Series A-B) establishing a customer-centric culture

**What you gain:**

- Direct access to strategic planning and board discussions
- Customer insights flow to the highest decision-making authority without being filtered
- Authority to challenge product decisions or pricing strategies that hurt customers
- A signal to the entire organization that customer outcomes matter as much as financial metrics

**What you risk:**

- CEO attention is scarce, and the relationship may become symbolic rather than functional
- The CS leader must have executive-level business acumen (not all do)
- Limited operational guidance from the CEO on day-to-day CS matters

![CEO, COO, and CRO Reporting Factor Comparison](images/Screenshot%202025-08-07%20at%2012.48.14.png)

*Figure 2.3: Reporting factor comparison across CEO, COO, and CRO structures*


**Fit indicators:**

- Expansion revenue below 20% of total growth
- Deep product-market fit refinement is needed
- Cross-functional influence requirements are high

---

**Model 2: COO Reporting, Operational Excellence**

**Best for:** Enterprise-stage organizations prioritizing consistency and scale

**What you gain:**

- Access to sophisticated operational frameworks and process documentation
- Standardized touchpoints that ensure consistent experience quality across the customer base
- Deep integration with implementation, support, and professional services
- A strong focus on resource optimization and efficiency

**What you risk:**

- A limited strategic voice to influence product development
- Difficulty demonstrating business impact in financial terms that the board values
- Process standardization may stifle creative problem-solving

**Fit indicators:**

- Large, diverse customer base requiring repeatable engagement
- Complex operational requirements across post-sale functions
- Cost-to-serve optimization is the primary concern

---

**Model 3: CRO Reporting, Revenue Engine Integration**

**Best for:** Growth-stage companies (Series C-D) where expansion exceeds 40% of revenue

**What you gain:**

- Clear revenue accountability (NRR, GRR, Expansion Revenue) with no ambiguity about whether CS is a cost centre or a growth driver
- Unified reporting that eliminates friction between Sales and CS
- Systematic identification of upsell and cross-sell opportunities
- Alignment with the growing trend in which 67% of CS executives now carry sales quotas

**What you risk:**

- Maintaining authentic customer advocacy under revenue pressure
- Cultural tensions with Sales teams over compensation and recognition
- The potential for short-term gains at the expense of long-term customer health

**Fit indicators:**

- Expansion exceeds 40% of total revenue growth
- The organization is transitioning from an acquisition-focused model to a retention-and-expansion model
- Revenue accountability for the CS function is being demanded by the board or investors

**Non-negotiable requirements:**

- CRO commitment to protecting customer advocacy
- Clear role delineation between Sales and CS
- Compensation rewarding *both* revenue outcomes and customer health

---

**Model 4: CCO Reporting, Dedicated Customer Leadership**

**Best for:** Large enterprises where experience differentiation is the primary competitive advantage

**What you gain:**

- Unified customer experience ownership across all post-sale touchpoints
- Dedicated C-suite advocacy for customer needs
- A long-term relationship focus unconstrained by quarterly revenue pressures
- Cross-functional coordination without revenue conflicts of interest

**What you risk:**

- An additional executive layer that adds complexity and cost
- Role clarity confusion with the CRO, COO, or product leadership
- The CCO may lack direct revenue accountability, which can reduce budget influence during tight fiscal cycles

**Fit indicators:**

- Expansion revenue above 30%
- Significant post-sale revenue from professional services
- Complex customer journeys that span multiple touchpoints and business units

**When Does the CCO Model Make Sense?**

The CCO role is most justified in organizations where the customer journey is long, multi-phased, and involves several internal teams whose priorities must be coordinated under a single executive. Companies with extensive professional services arms, complex onboarding programmes, and ongoing engagement models that span years rather than months find the CCO model effective because no other C-suite role can provide the same breadth of ownership. The model is also well suited to industries where customer experience itself is the differentiator, sectors such as enterprise healthcare technology, financial services platforms, and large-scale B2B marketplaces where the cost of churn is measured in millions of dollars per account. If your organization's post-sale revenue (renewals, expansions, and services combined) exceeds the revenue generated by new logo acquisition, a CCO can serve as the executive who bridges the gap between operational delivery and strategic growth, a gap that is left unaddressed when responsibility is split between a CRO focused on revenue targets and a COO focused on process efficiency.

The decision to hire a CCO should be weighed against the alternative of elevating the VP of Customer Success to report to the CEO. In many cases, the latter achieves the same strategic visibility at lower cost. The CCO model earns its place when the scope of customer ownership is too broad for a single VP to manage and when the organization's board has identified customer experience as a top-three strategic priority.

**How to Apply This in Your Organization**

**The 90-Day CS Placement Assessment**

**Phase 1: Organizational Assessment (Weeks 1-2)**

Calculate these metrics from your actual data:

| Metric | Calculation | Threshold |
| :----- | :----- | :----- |
| Expansion % of revenue growth | Expansion revenue ÷ Total revenue growth | \>40% suggests CRO reporting |
| Cross-functional dependencies | Count critical dependencies (Product, Sales, Marketing, Support, Finance) | \>5 suggests CEO or CCO |
| CS budget as % of ARR | Total CS budget ÷ ARR | Below 5-10% = under-resourced |

Survey your CS team on organizational influence and strategic impact. The answers will reveal whether the current structure is working or whether frustration is being masked by individual heroics.

---

🔧 **APPLY THIS TO YOUR ORG**

Pull your last four quarters of data. What percentage of total revenue growth came from expansion? If the number is above 40%, your CS team is functioning as a revenue team; the question is whether it has been structured and compensated like one.

---

**Phase 2: Structure Evaluation (Weeks 3-4)**

•	Score each reporting model against your assessment criteria

•	Model the financial impact using *your* data, not industry averages

•	Conduct stakeholder interviews to discover political landmines before they detonate

•	Develop a RACI matrix for customer-related decisions under each model

**Common Pitfalls and Edge Cases**

**Pitfall 1: Choosing Structure Based on Industry Trends, Not Your Context**

The 38% year-over-year growth in CRO reporting does not mean CRO reporting is right for you. A Series A company copying the structure of a Series D company will create chaos, not clarity.

**Edge case:** If you are early-stage but have high expansion revenue (some PLG companies fit this profile), CRO reporting may work despite the company's stage.

**Pitfall 2: Restructuring Without Adequate Change Management**

Seventy percent of organizational change initiatives fail due to inadequate change management. Prosci research confirms that the gap is real: initiatives with excellent change management achieve an **88% success rate**, while those with poor change management succeed just **13% of the time**. Technical restructuring without cultural adaptation fails. Every time.

**Timeline reality check:** Full implementation takes 7-12 months. Shorter timelines guarantee failure.

**Pitfall 3: Misaligned Incentives**

Nothing undermines structural changes faster than compensation that rewards old behaviours. If your CRM still routes accounts according to the old structure, people follow the system, not the org chart.

---

🔧 **APPLY THIS TO YOUR ORG**

Look at the compensation structure for your CS team. What percentage is tied to customer health metrics versus revenue metrics? If the weighting is heavy toward revenue, are you seeing signs that customer advocacy is being eroded, such as pushing upgrades that customers do not need?

---

**Pitfall 4: Underestimating the CEO Reporting Requirements**

CEO reporting sounds prestigious. But not all CS leaders have the strategic business acumen required for C-suite interaction. The CS leader must translate customer insights into board-level strategic language. An honest assessment is required: does your CS leader have this capability today, or is the gap one that can be closed through coaching within six months?

**Pitfall 5: Ignoring the Budget Reality**

Data shows 64% of CS teams operate with minimal non-headcount budgets of $200,000 or less per year. The reporting structure determines whether CS receives adequate investment. Teams buried several layers deep in sales operations are the ones that get under-resourced, their budgets treated as line items rather than strategic investments.

**Implementation Roadmap: The 7-12 Month Journey**

| Phase | Timeframe | Key Activities |
| :----- | :----- | :----- |
| **Stakeholder Alignment** | Months 1-2 | Secure executive sponsorship; identify change champions; develop a communication plan; create success metrics |
| **Restructuring** | Months 3-4 | Update job descriptions; create RACI matrices; adjust compensation; establish new meeting rhythms |
| **Process Adaptation** | Months 5-6 | Reconfigure the CRM; modify dashboards; refine playbooks; update escalation paths |
| **Optimization** | Months 7-12 | Conduct monthly performance reviews; run quarterly effectiveness assessments; make incremental adjustments based on the data |

**Change Management Checklist**

**Pre-Transition (30 days before):**

- \[ \] Written executive sponsorship from the CEO or Board
- \[ \] Stakeholder analysis identifying supporters, neutrals, and resistors
- \[ \] FAQ document addressing common concerns
- \[ \] Communication calendar with regular updates
- \[ \] Change champions briefed across all departments

**During Transition (Days 1-90):**

- \[ \] Town halls and AMAs scheduled for questions
- \[ \] One-on-one conversations conducted with all CS team members
- \[ \] All documentation, systems, and tools updated
- \[ \] Weekly transition team meetings held

**Post-Transition (Days 91-365):**

- \[ \] 30-60-90 day pulse surveys administered
- \[ \] Monthly performance reviews measured against the baseline
- \[ \] Quarterly retrospectives completed
- \[ \] Incremental adjustments documented and communicated

**Executive Summary & Questions to Ask Your Team**

**The Decision Framework in One Page**

**Your structure should optimize for:**

- **Strategy** (CEO): if you are early-stage or need deep product-market fit refinement
- **Operations** (COO): if you are at enterprise scale and prioritizing consistency
- **Revenue** (CRO): if expansion exceeds 40% of growth
- **Experience** (CCO): if experience differentiation is your competitive moat and the post-sale journey is too complex for a single VP to own

**Glossary of Key Terms**

| Term | Definition |
| :----- | :----- |
| **Change Management** | A structured approach to transitioning organizations from the current state to the desired state |
| **Chief Customer Officer (CCO)** | The C-suite executive responsible for unified customer experience strategy across all post-sale touchpoints |
| **Cost-to-Serve** | The total operational cost to support a customer or segment |
| **Expansion Revenue** | Additional revenue from existing customers (upsells, cross-sells, increased usage) |
| **Gross Renewal Rate** | The percentage of customers who renew contracts, measured before expansion revenue is added |
| **Net Revenue Retention (NRR)** | Revenue retained from existing customers including expansion, minus churn and contraction |
| **RACI Matrix** | A tool defining the Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, and Informed roles for each decision or process |
| **Strategic Elevation** | The positioning of a function at higher organizational levels to increase influence and visibility |
