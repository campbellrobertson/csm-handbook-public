**Opening Thesis**

**The automation era in Customer Success has delivered efficiency at the cost of trust, and trust is what retention is built on.** In the pursuit of scale, CSM practices have industrialized the customer journey, trading human intimacy for dashboards, health scores, and automated cadences. Churn is not a data problem. It is a trust problem. No algorithm, however sophisticated, has made a customer feel understood. PwC's 2025 Customer Experience Survey reveals that while nine in ten executives believe customer loyalty is rising, only four in ten consumers agree. That gap is not marginal; it is an organizational delusion, and it sits at the intersection of over-automation and metric misalignment. This chapter confronts that delusion and provides executives with a playbook for reintegrating human judgment into Customer Success without abandoning the operational gains of technology.

**Context & Stakes: Two CSM Organizations Face a Renewal Crisis**

Consider two Customer Success organizations managing the same scenario: a strategic account's champion has departed, and the new VP is evaluating competitors.

**Organization A's Response:** The health score remains green because product usage has not declined. The automated playbook triggers a standard check-in email. The CSM, who has never met the new VP, schedules a QBR featuring forty-seven slides of vendor-centric metrics. The renewal conversation opens cold, the competitor wins, and the post-mortem attributes the loss to "competitive pricing pressure."

**Organization B's Response:** The CSM detected the champion's departure through a relationship conversation two weeks before the CRM was updated. A trust-based introduction to the new VP was arranged through an adjacent stakeholder. The CSM conducted a Value Gap Review focused on the VP's strategic priorities, co-created a success plan aligned to the new agenda, and the renewal closed early with an expansion. The post-mortem attributes the win to "relationship depth."

The difference is not the technology stack. Both organizations use the same CSM platform. The difference is human judgment, relationship investment, and the organizational culture that enables them.

This scenario is not hypothetical. It plays out across thousands of renewal cycles each quarter, and the pattern is consistent: the accounts that churn are not the accounts with the worst product experience. They are the accounts where the human relationship was too thin to absorb the inevitable disruptions that every customer journey produces.

**Pillar 1: Why Humans Buy on Trust and Loyalty**

**The Psychology of B2B Decision-Making**

B2B purchasing decisions are perceived by most as rational exercises in risk assessment, vendor evaluation, and ROI calculation. This perception is misleading. Research from Google and CEB demonstrates that B2B buyers are close to 50% more likely to purchase a product or service when they perceive personal value, including feelings of being respected, confident, or appreciated. The emotional dimension of business buying is not a soft abstraction. It is a quantifiable commercial driver that shapes pipeline velocity, deal size, and renewal rates in ways that the standard sales funnel does not capture.

The 2025 Edelman Trust Barometer reinforces how trust operates at the peer level: 84% of business buyers trust friends and family, 80% trust customers like themselves, and only 58% trust CEOs. In a world saturated with vendor content, the most credible signal of trustworthiness comes not from platforms, dashboards, or automated NPS campaigns, but from other human beings. This finding carries profound implications for how CSM teams build and protect customer relationships.

The old adage persists: "You won't get fired if you buy IBM." In B2B markets, trust is not a bonus feature of the relationship. It is the structural precondition for every transaction, renewal, and expansion conversation that follows. The vendor who understands this builds relationships before building proposals, and the vendor who does not wonders, at renewal time, why the data looked so positive while the customer was already evaluating competitors.

**Emotional Loyalty Outperforms Transactional Loyalty**

Research from the Merkle Science of Loyalty study establishes that emotional loyalty, the authentic affinity consumers feel toward a brand, drives 65% more repeat purchases than transactional incentives alone. Loyalty programmes that focus on points, usage metrics, and system-generated health scores are building transactional loyalty by design. They are not building the kind of relationship resilience that sustains customers through product gaps, price increases, or competitive offers.

EY's Human Experience research makes this vivid: customers are 3.8 times more likely to make return purchases after a successful experience than after an unsuccessful one. The defining factor is not whether the product worked. It is whether the human experience of receiving support, guidance, and partnership felt authentic. This distinction between a successful product interaction and a successful human experience is where CSM automation falls short with the greatest frequency.

The practical implication for CSM leaders is clear. Points programmes, usage dashboards, and automated renewal reminders can maintain a floor of transactional engagement. But they cannot create the ceiling of emotional commitment that transforms a customer from a subscriber into an advocate. That transformation requires a human being who has invested enough in the relationship to understand why the customer's business matters to them, not just what the customer's usage patterns indicate.

**Risk Tolerance and the Trust Spectrum**

Not all customers occupy the same position on the trust spectrum, and effective CSM requires understanding this variation. Customer risk tolerance in technology adoption operates across two critical dimensions: how much risk a customer is willing to accept in a purchase decision, and how strategic the technology is to their business. High-risk-tolerance customers, those who are innovators and early adopters, may tolerate imperfect human engagement because they are motivated by exploration. Low-risk-tolerance customers, the majority of the enterprise market, require trust as the price of admission.

The shift from Fear of Missing Out (FOMO) to Fear of Messing Up (FOMU) in technology purchasing represents a structural change in the B2B trust environment. Where previous technology cycles were characterized by aggressive adoption, the current era demands demonstrable value and emotional confidence before decisions are made or renewed. CSM teams that rely on automated health scores and support ticket volumes to assess customer sentiment are blind to this emotional dimension of risk, the dimension that determines whether a renewal happens more often than any other.

The implications are practical. A CSM who understands where each account sits on the trust spectrum can calibrate their engagement model with precision that no algorithm achieves. The innovator who wants speed and access needs a different relationship than the risk-averse enterprise buyer who needs proof, reassurance, and a human voice on the other end of every high-stakes conversation.

**Pillar 2: The Automation Trap and What It Has Cost**

**A Decade of Industrialization**

The promise of automation in Customer Success was compelling: scale without proportional headcount, consistency without human variability, and data without subjective interpretation. The promise was not wrong. It was incomplete. The decade from 2015 to 2025 saw Customer Success Management undergo rapid industrialization. Platforms like Gainsight, Salesforce Success Cloud, and ChurnZero enabled CSM teams to manage hundreds of accounts through health scoring, automated playbooks, lifecycle triggers, and sentiment analysis. The efficiency gains were real. The unintended cost, a systematic erosion of human intimacy in customer relationships, was slower to surface but no less consequential.

Seventy percent of customers still prefer speaking to a human representative rather than a chatbot or digital assistant. Thirty-eight percent express frustration at having to repeat their concerns during a single interaction, a problem that automation compounds rather than resolves with high frequency. When automation substitutes for human judgment rather than supporting it, the experience fails at the level of trust. As practitioner Zino Okoro writes: "Automation without empathy may save money upfront but can lead to dissatisfied customers, increased escalations, and damage to a company's reputation. Customer retention is not about efficiency alone; it is about building meaningful relationships."

**The QBR Symptom**

Few practices illustrate the gap between CSM effort and customer value more than the Quarterly Business Review. Research and practitioner observation shows that CSMs spend more time preparing for QBRs than for any other customer interaction, yet customers arrive distracted, unprepared, and ready to leave within thirty minutes. Many customers are not too busy to engage. They are avoiding meetings they perceive as vendor-centric exercises with no value to them.

The anatomy of the failed QBR is revealing. Mediocre QBRs are monologues featuring forty-seven slides, vendor-centric framing, superficial data, and no action plan. They review what happened ninety days ago rather than addressing what matters right now. The customer who arrives at such a meeting does not feel heard. They feel processed. That experience does not build trust. It depletes it.

High-performing QBRs, by contrast, are dialogues built around five slides that serve as conversation starters, oriented around the customer's business outcomes, including meaningful feedback loops, and deriving a co-owned action plan. The difference is not the sophistication of the analytics platform behind the meeting. It is the quality of human preparation, listening, and judgment in the room.

| QBR Element | Mediocre Approach | High-Performing Approach |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Format | Monologue, 47 slides | Dialogue, 5 slides as conversation starters |
| Orientation | Vendor-centric metrics review | Customer business outcomes |
| Time horizon | Backward-looking (last 90 days) | Forward-looking (next quarter priorities) |
| Feedback | Superficial or absent | Structured feedback loop with candid input |
| Action plan | None or vendor-owned | Co-owned with clear accountability |
| Customer experience | Feels processed | Feels heard |

**Automating Activity, Not Outcomes**

One of the most revealing failures in CSM automation is the distinction between automating activity and automating outcomes. When automation systems launch without clear alignment to business outcomes, CSMs find themselves with hundreds of overdue tasks that bear no relationship to customer value. The platform has created motion without meaning. Customers notice. They experience automated outreach that feels formulaic, check-in calls that feel scripted, and health score improvements that mask unchanged business value.

As LinkedIn practitioner Nigel Hammond observes: traditional risk management monitors login metrics and schedules more check-in calls when usage drops. The health score improves, but business value remains unchanged. The value-driven approach identifies the unmet business need, collaborates to solve a real problem, and closes the value gap that puts the account at risk. The difference between these two approaches is not data access. It is human judgment about what the data means in the context of a specific customer's reality.

The cost of this confusion is measurable. Teams that automate activity report higher CSM capacity ratios and more completed playbook steps. They also report higher churn, lower NPS depth, and renewal conversations that feel transactional to the customer. The efficiency is real. So is the erosion.

**Pillar 3: The Trust Equation Applied to Customer Success**

**A Framework for Intentional Trust-Building**

The most powerful analytical framework for understanding how CSMs build and destroy trust comes from *The Trusted Advisor* by David Maister, Charles Green, and Robert Galford. The Trust Equation expresses trustworthiness as:

**T = (C + R + I) / S**

Where:

| Component | Speaks To | Definition |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **C (Credibility)** | Words | Can the CSM be believed based on credentials, expertise, and truthfulness? |
| **R (Reliability)** | Actions | Does the CSM follow through on commitments, large and small, with consistency? |
| **I (Intimacy)** | Emotional safety | Does the customer feel comfortable sharing real concerns, political sensitivities, and honest feedback? |
| **S (Self-Orientation)** | Motive (denominator) | Are all actions in service of the customer, or are they driven by the vendor's internal metrics, quotas, and renewal calendar? |

This equation reveals why automation-heavy CSM practices undermine trust in a systematic fashion. Automated systems can approximate Credibility by delivering accurate, timely product information. They can simulate Reliability through consistent cadence automation. But they cannot build Intimacy, because intimacy requires a human on the other side who cares about the customer's situation in an authentic way. And automation, by its nature, elevates Self-Orientation: when every interaction is triggered by a renewal timeline, a health score threshold, or a playbook step, customers perceive with accuracy that the engagement is organized around the vendor's interests, not their own.

As Charles Green explains: "Most humans buy from the heart and justify from the head." Credibility and Reliability satisfy the head. Intimacy and low Self-Orientation win the heart. A CSM organization that automates its way to efficient Credibility and Reliability while neglecting Intimacy has, in trust terms, amputated its most powerful differentiator.

**What AI Cannot Do**

The limits of AI in customer relationships are not technical. They are intrinsic to the nature of human judgment and moral agency. Judgment is the act of choosing among competing goods: short-term efficiency versus long-term loyalty, policy consistency versus compassionate exception, revenue protection versus brand goodwill. Models can estimate probabilities, but they cannot determine what a company should stand for in a dilemma.

A system can rank churn intent, flag low health scores, and recommend an intervention playbook. A person decides whether now is the moment to waive a fee for a customer who just lost a key stakeholder to illness, or to postpone a renewal conversation because the customer's CFO is managing a board crisis. These are not edge cases. They are the daily currency of trusted advisor relationships. They require empathy, context, and moral judgment, qualities that no language model possesses because they require care that is real, not the simulation of it.

The distinction matters because the cost of getting it wrong is asymmetric. A routine task handled by AI that could have been handled by a human wastes marginal efficiency. A high-stakes moment handled by AI that required a human destroys trust that took months to build and may never be rebuilt. The governance question is not whether to use AI. It is where the cost of failure demands human accountability.

The Forbes analysis of AI in customer experience establishes the structural principle: most CX failures happen when companies confuse the predictable with the emotional. When a problem is predictable and the customer is neutral in their emotions, AI should handle it. When the problem is ambiguous and the customer is charged with emotion, a human must ensure the outcome feels credible. The failure mode of over-automated CSM is not that AI handles too many routine tasks. It is that AI is deployed into moments of emotional significance where its lack of empathy produces the opposite of trust.

**Pillar 4: The Human Judgment Playbook**

**Where AI Serves, Where Humans Lead**

The reintegration of human judgment into CSM does not require abandoning automation. It requires strategic clarity about where automation enhances the human relationship and where it substitutes for it, to the detriment of all involved. The Canadian Marketing Association's 2025 Human-AI Collaboration framework articulates the governing principle: effective human-AI collaboration requires deploying AI assistance and human judgment where each adds the most value.

The following allocation model provides executives with a decision framework for every customer interaction type:

| Interaction Type | Customer Emotion | Business Complexity | Recommended Actor | Rationale |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Routine onboarding steps | Neutral | Low | AI-assisted automation | Speed and consistency valued |
| Usage alert / feature nudge | Neutral | Low | Automated with human review | Scale efficiency, low trust risk |
| Health score deterioration | Mixed | Medium | Human-led investigation | Interpretation requires context |
| Strategic business review | Engaged | High | Human-led with AI preparation | Trust, intimacy, and judgment required |
| Renewal negotiation | Variable | High | Human-led, AI-informed | Emotional and commercial complexity |
| Executive escalation | Charged | High | Human only | Moral agency and brand integrity |
| Competitive threat signal | Anxious | High | Human-led, urgent | Trust is on the line |
| Champion departure / org change | Uncertain | High | Human-led, immediate | Relationship continuity is existential |

**Signals That Only Humans Detect**

What does effective human listening surface that dashboards miss? The most important churn signals are organizational and relational rather than behavioural:

- **Loss of champion:** The internal advocate who sponsored the purchase has left, been promoted out of scope, or become marginalized in a political sense. This signal almost never appears in product usage data but is one of the strongest predictors of non-renewal.
- **Strategic priority shift:** The customer's business context has changed through a merger, a new CEO, or a board-mandated cost reduction, and the product no longer serves the new agenda. This surfaces in executive conversations, not ticket queues.
- **Confidence deficit:** The customer has private doubts about whether they made the right purchase decision, but they are not voicing these concerns because the relationship does not feel safe for honesty. This emerges through Intimacy alone, the third element of the Trust Equation.
- **Relationship debt:** Accumulated minor disappointments, the unreturned call, the cancelled executive sponsor meeting, the QBR that added no value, have built a quiet resentment that will crystallize at renewal time.

None of these signals are captured with reliability by Medallion reports or support ticket analysis. All of them are accessible to a CSM who has invested in the human relationship with enough depth and regularity that the customer shares their real situation.

**Rehumanizing the Customer Journey: Six Practical Interventions**

The following playbook elements represent concrete ways to reintroduce human judgment into standard CSM motions:

**1. The Discovery Conversation at Onboarding.** Before deploying a single automated workflow, the CSM should conduct a structured discovery conversation to understand not just what outcomes the customer purchased the product to achieve, but their organizational context, decision-making dynamics, risk tolerance, and definition of success. This conversation is the trust deposit that all subsequent automated touchpoints will draw upon.

**2. The Value Gap Review (Replacing the Standard QBR).** Rather than preparing a forty-slide vendor retrospective, the CSM co-creates a Value Gap Review: a short, dialogue-based meeting oriented around the customer's unrealized business outcomes. Three questions sent five days in advance, "What has shifted in your priorities?", "What does success look like next quarter?", "What should we do in a different way?", convert a vendor monologue into a customer-owned strategic conversation.

**3. The Trust Equation Account Audit.** On a quarterly basis, the CSM should evaluate each strategic account against the four components of the Trust Equation: Where is credibility strong or weak? Have all commitments been honoured? Is there real intimacy, meaning real knowledge of the customer's business context and personal concerns? Is the relationship centred on the customer or organized around the vendor's renewal calendar?

**4. The Champion Map and Stakeholder Relationship Review.** A living map of organizational relationships, documenting who the current champions are, who influences the renewal decision, and who has changed roles, maintained through human conversation rather than CRM fields. The detection of champion risk is a human intelligence function at its core.

**5. Human Ownership of Peak Moments.** Every customer journey has peak moments: the critical incident, the major product failure, the organizational change, the near-churn recovery. The peak-end rule from behavioural psychology demonstrates that customers judge entire relationships by their peak moment and their final interaction. These moments must be owned by humans with the authority to make exceptions, express authentic accountability, and take actions that no automated playbook would prescribe.

**6. Governance: Locking High-Stakes Decisions Behind Human Review.** Automation systems should be deployed in monitored mode for 30-90 days, with false positives and negatives measured by account tier. High-impact decisions, any automated action on a strategic account, should require human review before execution. Explainability fields should be required for every model alert, and rollback procedures must allow a human to cancel any automated sequence if context has changed.

These six interventions are not a wish list. They are a minimum viable human engagement framework. The organizations that implement all six create a compounding trust advantage that grows stronger with each quarter, because every human interaction done well deposits trust that subsequent automated touchpoints can draw upon without depleting.

**Pillar 5: The Executive Accountability Imperative**

**Metrics That Lie and Metrics That Tell the Truth**

The organizational pressure to automate is driven in part by the availability of metrics that reward automation: health scores improve, playbook completion rates increase, CSM capacity ratios expand, and executive reporting looks healthy. These are metrics of activity, not outcomes. They can improve while customer trust deteriorates, champion relationships erode, and renewal conversations grow more transactional with each passing quarter.

The danger is that these activity metrics create a false sense of security at the executive level. The board sees green dashboards and rising efficiency ratios. The CSMs on the ground see customers who are disengaging in ways that no metric captures: shorter emails, fewer questions, polite but empty QBR attendance, and an absence of the candid feedback that once characterized the relationship. By the time the health score turns red, the trust erosion is complete and the decision to leave has been made.

Executives need a complementary set of human relationship metrics that measure what automation cannot:

| Metric | What It Measures | Why It Matters |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Net Promoter Depth** | Richness and specificity of qualitative commentary from strategic accounts | Distinguishes substantive, outcome-focused feedback from generic responses |
| **Executive Engagement Quality** | Frequency and depth of CSM access to customer executive sponsors | Measured by conversation content, not meeting counts |
| **Champion Relationship Index** | Strength, breadth, and organizational depth of human relationships within each account | Predicts renewal resilience when organizational changes occur |
| **Value Gap Closure Rate** | Percentage of identified business value gaps closed through tracked interventions | Separates strategic value delivery from activity completion |
| **Renewal Conversation Health** | Qualitative assessment of trust and relationship standing | Distinct from health score, reflects human intelligence |

**Culture Before Capability**

Reintroducing human judgment into CSM is not a technology or process challenge at its core. It is a cultural one. Organizations that have hired CSMs as platform administrators, have incentivized them on health score metrics, and have measured their performance through playbook completion rates have trained a generation of CSMs who are excellent at tasks and inexperienced at relationships. Rebuilding this capability requires cultural investment made with intent: hiring for emotional intelligence alongside technical knowledge, rewarding relationship depth alongside operational efficiency, and creating organizational permission to invest time in human conversations that do not produce an immediate metric.

The CSM of the future is not a platform administrator or a metrics manager. The CSM of the future will be embedded inside their customers' workflows and teams, a strategic partner who understands the customer's business with greater depth than any automated system can, and who is trusted to tell the customer what they need to hear, not what the dashboard shows.

PwC's data on data privacy adds a further dimension: while 53% of consumers find personalization valuable enough to share data, 93% say a brand will lose their trust if that data is mishandled. Every personalization strategy built on customer data carries a built-in trust trigger. The CSM organization that relies on data-driven insights without corresponding human transparency, explaining what data is used and why, risks converting a relationship asset into a liability.

**Common Pitfalls and Edge Cases**

**Pitfall 1: The Green Health Score Delusion**

**The mistake:** Trusting automated health scores as proxies for relationship health. **The result:** Accounts with strong product usage but weak human relationships churn without warning, and the post-mortem reveals signals that were invisible to the platform but obvious to anyone with a relationship in the account. **The fix:** Layer human relationship metrics on top of product health scores. A green health score with a red Champion Relationship Index is a high-risk account.

**Pitfall 2: Automating High-Stakes Moments**

**The mistake:** Allowing automated playbooks to govern interactions during executive escalations, competitive threats, or organizational changes. **The result:** The customer receives a templated response during a moment that demands human empathy, and the trust deficit becomes irreversible. **The fix:** Lock all high-stakes interactions behind human review. Build explicit governance rules that route emotional and complex situations to human CSMs with the authority to act.

**Pitfall 3: The One-Size-Fits-All Journey**

**The mistake:** Designing a single automation journey across the entire customer base without regard for automation readiness. **The result:** High-touch customers receive impersonal digital engagement, eroding trust that was built through human relationships. Tech-Touch customers receive more human contact than they need, reducing efficiency without increasing value. **The fix:** Segment by automation readiness alongside ARR tier. Design distinct journeys for high-touch, mid-touch, and Tech-Touch customers.

**Pitfall 4: Measuring Activity Instead of Outcomes**

**The mistake:** Reporting on playbook completion rates, email open rates, and meeting counts as indicators of CSM success. **The result:** The team optimizes for volume rather than value, and the metrics look strong while customer trust weakens. **The fix:** Shift measurement to outcome-based metrics: value gaps closed, executive relationships deepened, and renewal conversations initiated from a position of trust rather than obligation.

**Pitfall 5: Ignoring the Data Privacy Trust Trigger**

**The mistake:** Building personalization strategies on customer data without transparent communication about what is collected and how it is used. **The result:** According to PwC, 93% of consumers say a brand will lose their trust if data is mishandled, and the trust breach spreads faster than any retention programme can contain. **The fix:** Pair every data-driven insight with human transparency. The CSM should be able to explain, in plain language, what data informed a recommendation and why the customer should view that as value rather than surveillance.

**Edge Cases**

**AI-native customers:** Some customers, in particular those in the technology sector, may prefer AI-driven engagement as their default. Segment these accounts with care and offer the choice, but maintain human availability for high-stakes moments regardless of customer preference.

**Post-acquisition integration:** When a customer is acquired by a new parent company, every relationship must be rebuilt from the foundation. Automated continuity plans fail because the people have changed. Human-led re-discovery is required.

**Single-threaded accounts:** Where only one contact exists, the entire relationship is existential risk. The human priority is multi-threading: building relationships with additional stakeholders before the single thread breaks.

**Regulated industries:** Customers in healthcare, financial services, and government operate under compliance constraints that demand human accountability for every decision. Automated actions in these environments carry legal and reputational risk that no playbook can absorb. Human governance is not optional; it is a regulatory requirement.

**If You Remember Only One Thing...**

**Trust is built by humans, and retention is built on trust.** The data is unambiguous: 52% of consumers stop buying after a bad experience, 38% say AI has made service worse, and only 3% of companies are customer-obsessed in a way that research can verify. Health scores measure product engagement, not relationship health. Medallion reports describe yesterday. The quiet erosion of trust that drives next quarter's churn is visible only to a CSM who has invested in the human relationship with enough depth that the customer shares what is real. Automation should handle the predictable so that humans can own the emotional, the complex, and the strategic. The organizations that internalize this truth, that rebuild the human foundation of their Customer Success practice, will capture the 25-95% profit improvement that a 5% retention gain delivers. The organizations that do not will watch their best customers leave without warning, convinced to the end that their health scores looked fine.

**Glossary of Key Terms**

| Term | Definition |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Automation Readiness** | A customer's tolerance for digital-first, automated engagement in the CSM relationship, distinct from account size or ARR tier |
| **Champion Map** | A living organizational intelligence document tracking the identities, relationships, and risk status of key human advocates within a customer account |
| **Emotional Loyalty** | The authentic affinity a customer feels toward a brand or vendor, driven by positive relational experiences and a sense of being understood, valued, and partnered with; distinguished from transactional loyalty |
| **FOMU (Fear of Messing Up)** | The risk-averse orientation in B2B technology purchasing that has replaced FOMO as the dominant behavioural driver in enterprise technology adoption decisions |
| **Health Score** | An algorithmic metric generated by CSM platforms that aggregates product usage, engagement, and support data to produce a single indicator of account risk |
| **Human Judgment** | The capacity to weigh competing values, interpret contextual nuance, and make decisions that integrate moral, relational, and strategic considerations |
| **Intimacy (Trust Equation)** | The third component of the Trust Equation; the degree to which a customer feels safe in an emotional sense sharing real concerns |
| **Medallion Reports** | Automated vendor-generated reports including usage data, health scores, and product adoption metrics |
| **Peak-End Rule** | A principle from behavioural psychology demonstrating that people judge an experience by its most intense moment and its final moment |
| **Risk Tolerance (Customer)** | The degree to which a B2B customer is willing to accept uncertainty in their technology decisions |
| **Self-Orientation (Trust Equation)** | The denominator in the Trust Equation; the degree to which the CSM's actions are organized around their own metrics rather than the customer's outcomes |
| **Trust Equation** | A model of trustworthiness developed by David Maister, Charles Green, and Robert Galford: T = (C + R + I) / S |
| **Trusted Advisor** | A CSM relationship archetype in which the customer views the CSM as a strategic partner and proactive guide |
| **Value Gap** | The distance between the business outcomes a customer purchased a product to achieve and the outcomes being realized at present |
| **Value Gap Review (VGR)** | An alternative to the traditional QBR, structured as a brief dialogue-based meeting co-owned by the customer, focused on unrealized business outcomes |
